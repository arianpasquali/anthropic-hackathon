# Klimaatkracht — A Sponsored-Impact Marketplace for Food Rescue

*Pitch storyline, 5-minute hackathon demo format*

---

## Situation

Food banks in the Netherlands serve 155,600 people — and reach only 15–17% of those who actually qualify for help. Demand grew 7.5% last year. Voedselbanken Nederland operates 172 chapters across the country, almost entirely on volunteer labor, with chronic funding shortfalls that force them to turn families away.

What's less well known is that those same food banks are quietly one of the country's most efficient climate operators. The Food Bank of Navarra in Spain published the math in 2022: their operations have a 32-to-1 carbon ratio — for every tonne of CO2e they emit running trucks and refrigerators, they prevent 32 tonnes from reaching landfill. The Global Foodbanking Network puts the global figure at 1.8 million tonnes of CO2e prevented annually across its 50-country member network. Translated to Voedselbanken Nederland's scale: roughly 80,000–120,000 tonnes of CO2e avoided every year — comparable to taking 50,000 cars off the road.

So food banks are simultaneously the most underfunded social infrastructure in the country and one of its most underrated climate assets.

## Complication

Meanwhile, in another building across town, the corporate sustainability manager at a Dutch mid-sized company is having a worse week than usual. The CSRD reporting deadline is approaching, the board wants climate credentials in the annual report, and the head of marketing wants something for the website that doesn't sound like greenwashing. Their options are bleak.

They can buy carbon credits on the voluntary market — but the Verra and Gold Standard rainforest scandals have made the auditing committee nervous, and the EU's anti-greenwashing directive has lawyers warning against any offset-based claims. They can pay a consultancy to write a fluffy report — expensive and increasingly mocked. They can fund a generic CSR partnership — but their CEO wants verifiable numbers, not a logo on someone else's website.

What they actually want — verifiable climate impact, with social co-benefits, with audit-ready evidence, in the Netherlands, by Q4 — does not exist as a product they can buy.

So one organization desperately needs funding. Another organization desperately wants to fund verifiable climate impact. They cannot find each other.

## Opportunity

We're building Klimaatkracht — a sponsored-impact marketplace that converts food banks' climate contribution into a revenue stream they would otherwise never access.

The mechanic is simple. Corporates buy Climate-Action Packages — €25,000 sponsors a verified 600 tonnes of CO2e avoided through a specific Dutch food bank, with quarterly audit-ready reports they can drop directly into their CSR materials. Food banks receive funds. Corporates receive verifiable impact backed by methodology aligned with FRAME — the Global Foodbanking Network's Gold Standard-track measurement framework.

What makes this work technically is real-time verification at the loading dock. A volunteer scans a barcode or photographs a crate; computer vision classifies and weighs the donation; the system applies peer-reviewed CO2e coefficients and aggregates the impact in real-time. AI handles three layers of work that no human could do at this scale: vision-based intake classification of the messy reality of food donations, automated generation of buyer-specific impact reports tuned to each corporate's CSR framework, and anti-fraud checks that prevent double-counting between our claims and the donating retailer's own ESG accounting.

What makes it defensible commercially is the price arbitrage. A Verra carbon credit goes for €5–€30 per tonne and takes 18 months to certify. Sponsored-impact, sold directly to corporates as CSR-grade verifiable reporting, commands €40–€80 per tonne and ships next quarter. The value isn't tradeable — it's reputational — and that's exactly what corporate buyers actually want.

We're not selling carbon credits. We're selling something corporates need more: verifiable climate impact with poverty co-benefits, in their backyard, that doesn't get them sued for greenwashing. The food banks that already exist, doing the work they already do, become the supply side of a market that isn't currently being served.

The Dutch numbers, conservatively: 100,000 tonnes of annual CO2e impact across Voedselbanken Nederland, sold at €50 per tonne sponsored-impact, generates €5 million in new annual revenue — directly to food banks, ring-fenced for operations they choose. That's roughly a third of the sector's current funding gap, unlocked from a budget category nobody knew applied to them.

## Judge Bias

**Jacqueline van den Ende — Founder, Carbon Equity.**

Jacqueline has spent her career proving that climate impact can be made financially legible — turning what was philanthropy into investible asset classes. Carbon Equity democratised access to climate-investment funds; Klimaatkracht does the analogous translation for nonprofits. Her question will be: "Is the methodology defensible?" The answer is FRAME, the same Gold Standard-track methodology the Global Foodbanking Network is using globally, applied without the certification overhead because we're not selling on registries. She's also the buyer most likely to catch additionality and double-counting risks — and we should signal we've thought about both. The pitch lands with her if it reads as climate-finance infrastructure rather than CSR theatre.

**Clare Jones — CEO, Polarsteps.**

Clare runs a consumer product where the moment of value is *making the invisible visible* — a year of travel becomes a single beautiful map. Klimaatkracht does the same operation for an entirely different domain: a year of food bank operations becomes a single legible climate contribution. Her instinct will be product polish — does the corporate buyer's experience feel like a product or like nonprofit software? Does the volunteer's intake interface feel like Polarsteps' map or like a clipboard PDF? We need to demo the loading-dock scan and the buyer report and have both feel like products people would choose to use. She's also the most likely to push on emotional resonance: this isn't a B2B SaaS, it's the bridge between two strangers who didn't know they needed each other.

**Ruben Timmermann — School of Moral Ambition.**

Two of Ruben's three priorities are food systems. Twenty years of fundraising experience means he's seen every failed nonprofit funding model. He knows the structural problem with food bank financing better than anyone in the room: stigma-laden, project-funded, perpetually one grant cycle from crisis. The pitch that lands with him is the one that solves the structural problem — recurring corporate revenue, ring-fenced to operations, scaled by activity rather than by grant-application luck. He's also the judge most likely to ask the moral-ambition question: "Why hasn't anyone done this already?" The honest answer is that food banks have been treated as charity, not infrastructure — and you can't sell a sponsored-impact product on top of charity. You can sell it on top of infrastructure. This pitch reframes the sector.

---

## What lands across all three

The same product reads differently to each judge, and that's a feature: Jacqueline sees climate-finance infrastructure, Clare sees a polished two-sided product, Ruben sees structural change in the funding model of a sector he's spent his life trying to fix. None of them needs to be convinced of the same thing.

The hackathon demo needs to show the round-trip — corporate buyer purchases a package, food bank receives the commitment, intake happens at the dock, AI generates the impact report, corporate buyer reads it. Five minutes, three audiences, one mechanism. The food banks already produce the climate impact. We just made it sellable.
