# Open Problems in the Non-Profit and Emergency Services Sectors

**Prepared for:** Build Local Hackathon, Amsterdam, April 25–26
**Date:** April 20, 2026
**Scope:** Netherlands / Benelux / Europe (with global references where useful)
**Methodology:** Each problem is described in terms of (a) what it is, (b) why it exists, (c) who it affects and how, and (d) credible sources. Problems carry stable IDs (NP-## for non-profits, ES-## for emergency services) used across subsequent documents in this pipeline.

## Part I — Non-Profit Sector Problems

### NP-01. Chronic funding shortfalls and operating deficits

**Description.** A majority of nonprofits cannot raise enough to cover their operating costs. Independent Sector's 2025 *Health of the U.S. Nonprofit Sector* reports that 81% of surveyed organisations struggled to raise enough to cover all costs, and 36% ended the most recent fiscal year in deficit; in parallel, a third reported disruptions to government funding in the first 4–6 months of 2025.

**Cause.** A structural scissors-crunch: demand is rising (68% expect service demand to grow in 2026) while grant cycles shorten, individual giving contracts post-COVID, and government budgets retrench. In the Netherlands specifically, a new national action plan is being drafted amid proposed unemployment benefit cuts that the food-bank sector warns will push more people below the subsistence line.

**Effect.** Program cuts, waiting lists, layoffs, and mission drift. Smaller organisations serving marginalised communities are disproportionately exposed because they lack endowment buffers. At food banks in the Netherlands, the combined effect of the 2024–2025 cost-of-living shock was a 7.5% year-over-year jump to 155,600 people served.

**Sources.** Independent Sector, *2025 Health of the U.S. Nonprofit Sector* (https://independentsector.org/blog/nonprofits-squeezed-growing-community-need-fewer-resources/); NL Times, "Food bank use in Netherlands jumps 7.5% to 155,600" (https://nltimes.nl/2026/03/09/food-bank-use-netherlands-jumps-75-155600-amid-rising-cost-living); Forvis Mazars, *2025 State of the Nonprofit Sector Report* (https://www.forvismazars.us/forsights/2025/02/2025-state-of-the-nonprofit-sector-report).

### NP-02. Administrative burden of grant applications disproportionate to grant size

**Description.** Grant application and reporting workload correlates poorly with award size: GrantFlow reports that $2M grants can require one-twentieth the effort of $2,500 grants. Development-staff turnover averages 16–18 months, and there is no national credentialing pathway for grant writers.

**Cause.** Funders design bespoke application frameworks; small organisations twist existing programs to fit each new framework; no standardisation across funders. EU grants compound this with fragmented national rules and comparability tests across member states (burden of proof on the NPO).

**Effect.** Grassroots organisations are systematically outcompeted by larger orgs with dedicated development teams. Competent programs remain underfunded. It takes on average 2 years 4 months before a new nonprofit receives its first grant, and 86% of nonprofits pursuing government/foundation grants will not receive impactful amounts.

**Sources.** Stanford Social Innovation Review, "Nonprofits Need Grant Writing Infrastructure" (https://ssir.org/articles/entry/grant-writing-infrastructure); NonProfit PRO, "Study Identifies Gap Between Grant Availability, Nonprofits' Needs" (https://www.nonprofitpro.com/article/study-significant-gap-between-availability-of-grant-funding-nonprofits-financial-needs/); European Parliament study, *A Statute for European Cross-Border Associations and Non-Profit Organisations* (https://www.europarl.europa.eu/meetdocs/2014_2019/plmrep/COMMITTEES/JURI/DV/2021/05-11/).

### NP-03. Donor retention decline and changing giving behaviour

**Description.** Individual giving is falling faster than any other revenue source. Meta and other platforms have tightened or banned nonprofit social-issue ads under the EU TTPA regulation, damaging donor engagement and acquisition funnels. In the Netherlands, direct-marketing channels now face stricter consumer-protection rules.

**Cause.** Demographic shifts (fewer recurring donors, younger cohorts donate differently), advertising platform policy changes, regulatory tightening on telemarketing, declining trust in foundations (38% high trust vs. 57% for nonprofits), and the absence of robust digital-first acquisition tooling in smaller orgs.

**Effect.** Nonprofits lose their most flexible and unrestricted revenue source — the one that pays for overhead and innovation. Organisations with weak digital maturity feel this hardest and cannot pivot quickly.

**Sources.** EFA, "Fundraising in Europe: 2025 in review" (https://efa-net.eu/features/fundraising-in-europe-2025-in-review-expectations-for-the-year-ahead/); Forvis Mazars, *2025 State of the Nonprofit Sector Report*; Independent Sector, *2025 Health of the U.S. Nonprofit Sector*.

### NP-04. Digital maturity gap: most nonprofits stuck below operational effectiveness

**Description.** The 2022 Nonprofit Trends Report (Salesforce) found that 74% of nonprofit leaders agree digital transformation is important, but only 12% have achieved "digital maturity." 88% of nonprofits are stuck.

**Cause.** Limited funding for technology (nearly half of small charities cite funding as the top barrier vs. 27% of large charities), leadership resistance, siloed data, legacy workflows, and an inability to translate technical capabilities into strategic decisions.

**Effect.** Measurable performance gap: advanced-analytics nonprofits reported revenue increases while less mature peers stagnate. Beneficiaries are served less efficiently; smaller and rural orgs fall further behind urban peers.

**Sources.** Nonprofit Quarterly, "Digital Transformation, a Nonprofit Primer" (https://nonprofitquarterly.org/digital-transformation-a-nonprofit-primer/); Small Charities Data, "Small charities and digital technology" (https://smallcharitiesdata.org/topic/small-charities-and-digital-technology/); Godefroid & Plattfaut (2024), "Identifying key barriers to nonprofit organizations' adoption of technology innovations" *Nonprofit Management and Leadership* (https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1002/nml.21609).

### NP-05. Volunteer recruitment shortfall

**Description.** 46.8% of nonprofit leaders cite volunteer recruitment as a top challenge. U.S. Census data shows formal volunteering fell from 30% pre-COVID (2017, 2019) to 28% post-COVID (2023), and 75% of nonprofits consider volunteers important or essential to operations.

**Cause.** Changing motivations (episodic vs. long-term commitments), fragmentation of community life, digital burnout, lack of marketing budget for recruitment, mismatch between role descriptions and volunteer interests, and the absence of efficient matching infrastructure.

**Effect.** Event cancellations, reduced service coverage, burnout of remaining volunteers, and increased staff workload. Small organisations without recruitment technology cannot compete for volunteer attention.

**Sources.** ASU Lodestar, "Using Motivations for Volunteer Recruitment & Retention" (https://lodestar.asu.edu/blog/2024/04/using-motivations-volunteer-recruitment-and-retention-strategy); Independent Sector, *2025 Health of the U.S. Nonprofit Sector*; Urban Institute, *Volunteer Management Practices and Retention of Volunteers* (https://www.urban.org/sites/default/files/publication/58001/411005-Volunteer-Management-Practices-and-Retention-of-Volunteers.PDF).

### NP-06. Volunteer retention and disorganised coordination

**Description.** Volunteer turnover disrupts operations and signals that the experience is not rewarding. Common failure modes: scattered communications across email / spreadsheets / chats, unclear expectations, weak recognition, and poor role-interest fit.

**Cause.** Nonprofits typically invest in acquisition but not in ongoing engagement. Management overhead falls on already-stretched staff. Few organisations have software supporting lifecycle management of volunteers.

**Effect.** High turnover means constant onboarding; skilled volunteers leave; programs collapse when a single informal lead departs. Studies show role-clarity, written descriptions and explicit expectations improve retention materially, but adoption is uneven.

**Sources.** Nonprofit Learning Lab, "How to Address Volunteer Management Challenges and Improve Retention" (https://www.nonprofitlearninglab.org/post/how-to-address-volunteer-management-challenges-and-improve-retention); AmeriCorps, *Engaging Volunteers: A Comprehensive Literature Review* (https://www.americorps.gov/sites/default/files/document/Literature%20Review%20for%20Volunteer%20Management_0.pdf).

### NP-07. Impact measurement complexity and reporting burden

**Description.** Nonprofits struggle to identify a short list of outcomes that are both meaningful and measurable. Many rely on rudimentary tools like Excel, conflate outputs with outcomes, over-collect data they cannot use, and cannot establish causality of their interventions.

**Cause.** Lack of technical capacity, absence of standard outcome taxonomies, funder-imposed custom metrics, limited budget for evaluation specialists, and insufficient participatory-design involvement of beneficiaries.

**Effect.** Funders lose confidence; programs cannot iterate based on evidence; beneficiaries' voices are underrepresented; reporting becomes compliance theatre instead of learning.

**Sources.** Bridgespan, *A Practical Guide to Nonprofit Measurement, Evaluation, and Learning* (https://www.bridgespan.org/insights/nonprofit-organizational-effectiveness/a-practical-guide-to-nonprofit-measurement-evaluation-and-learning); funds for NGOs, "The Challenges of Measuring Social Impact for Nonprofits" (https://us.fundsforngos.org/articles/the-challenges-of-measuring-social-impact-for-nonprofits/); Virtuous Software, "The 8 Reasons Why Nonprofits Measure Impact" (https://virtuous.org/blog/the-8-reasons-why-nonprofits-measure-impact/).

### NP-08. Staff burnout and compensation gap

**Description.** The Center for Effective Philanthropy's 2025 *State of Nonprofits* reports widespread burnout, disproportionately affecting staff of colour and staff from historically marginalised groups. Only 12% of sub-$250k-budget orgs offer health insurance, and more than one in ten organisations reports at least 21% of staff positions vacant.

**Cause.** Flat wages in an inflationary environment, expanding workload as demand rises, emotional weight of mission-aligned work, political hostility toward beneficiaries, and weak career ladders inside small orgs.

**Effect.** High attrition, loss of institutional knowledge, compromised service delivery, and a feedback loop where overworked staff cannot invest in retention or modernisation.

**Sources.** Center for Effective Philanthropy, *State of Nonprofits 2025* (https://cep.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/NVP_State-of-Nonprofits_2025.pdf); Independent Sector, *2025 Health of the U.S. Nonprofit Sector*.

### NP-09. Senior leadership and specialist recruitment difficulty

**Description.** More than three out of four nonprofits report difficulty recruiting senior leaders, and about two-thirds have no leadership mentoring program. Small-org talent pipelines are thin.

**Cause.** Compensation disparity with private sector, board governance weaknesses, limited succession planning, concentration of experience in large urban organisations, and insufficient professional development infrastructure in the sector.

**Effect.** Critical leadership vacancies, reliance on interim leaders, strategic drift, and loss of funder relationships that often attach to individuals.

**Sources.** Forvis Mazars, *2025 State of the Nonprofit Sector Report*; Oxford HR, "Navigating the Evolving Funding Landscape" (https://oxfordhr.com/thought-leadership/navigating-the-evolving-funding-landscape-challenges-and-future-trends-for-non-profit-organisations/).

### NP-10. Cybersecurity vulnerability to ransomware, phishing, and state actors

**Description.** Only 1 in 10 NGOs trains its staff regularly on cybersecurity, only 1 in 4 monitors its networks, and only 1 in 5 has a cybersecurity plan. 41% of NGOs surveyed by the CyberPeace Institute reported cyberattacks in the last three years.

**Cause.** Budget scarcity forces deprioritisation, cloud transition expands attack surface, shared credentials and unmanaged identities proliferate, beneficiary-facing apps collect sensitive data without commensurate protection, and small orgs lack vCISO-level strategic guidance.

**Effect.** The 2022 ICRC breach affected 515,000+ people whose family-reunification data was exposed; refugees and activists face real-world danger when their data is leaked; ransomware downtime delays time-sensitive aid.

**Sources.** CyberPeace Institute, *NGOs serving Humanity at risk: Cyber Threats affecting "International Geneva"* (https://geneva.cyberpeace.ngo/); ICRC, "Safeguarding humanitarian organizations from digital threats" (https://blogs.icrc.org/law-and-policy/2022/10/13/safeguarding-humanitarian-organizations-from-digital-threats/); CTA, *Cyber Threats to NGOs 2024* (https://www.cyberthreatalliance.org/wp-content/uploads/2024/04/Cyber-Threats-to-NGOs_FINAL.pdf).

### NP-11. Cross-border EU regulatory and legal compliance burden

**Description.** NPOs operating across EU borders face different civil and tax regimes per member state, with the burden of proving comparability falling on them. Foundations incur €90M–€101.7M in annual cross-border compliance costs.

**Cause.** Absence of a harmonised EU statute for nonprofit associations; national authorities exercise broad discretion with case-by-case evidentiary requirements; language and administrative differences between member states.

**Effect.** Small NPOs are shut out of pan-European activity; donors cannot easily give across borders with tax efficiency; coalitions that could tackle problems at EU scale remain stuck at national level.

**Sources.** European Parliament, *A Statute for European Cross-Border Associations and Non-Profit Organisations* (https://www.europarl.europa.eu/meetdocs/2014_2019/plmrep/COMMITTEES/JURI/DV/2021/05-11/).

---

### NP-12. Platform policy changes restricting nonprofit ad reach

**Description.** Major social media platforms have enforced strict rules on paid ads, with some (Meta) limiting nonprofit messaging to an extent that affects freedom of expression, donor income, and supporter engagement. The EU Transparency and Targeting of Political Advertising regulation has had unintended consequences for civic-space organisations.

**Cause.** Platforms' classification of advocacy and social-issue content as political, auto-blocking legitimate nonprofit outreach; regulatory overcorrection toward political advertising.

**Effect.** Drop in donor-acquisition efficiency, narrower public education capacity, and a shift of resources away from mission delivery toward platform appeals and compliance.

**Sources.** EFA, "Fundraising in Europe: 2025 in review" (https://efa-net.eu/features/fundraising-in-europe-2025-in-review-expectations-for-the-year-ahead/).

### NP-13. Food banks reach only 15–17% of eligible population

**Description.** Per CBS and Nibud figures, Dutch food banks reach only 15–17% of people who could potentially qualify for support. Voedselbank Leiden, which supports 1,200 people weekly, is an example of this coverage gap.

**Cause.** Stigma and dignity concerns deter potential clients; eligibility criteria based on income after fixed costs are bureaucratic and poorly understood; limited awareness; geographic clustering of banks; language barriers for immigrant communities.

**Effect.** Families in poverty go without nutritional support despite the availability of food aid; the official poverty rate understates the problem; policy debates proceed on partial data.

**Sources.** Stichting De Groentedeler, "Behind the scenes at Voedselbank Leiden" (https://www.degroentedeler.nl/en/behind-the-scenes-food-bank/); Leiden International Centre, "Food Banks in the Netherlands" (https://www.leideninternationalcentre.nl/get-advice/blogs/food-banks-in-the-netherlands); Voedselbanken Nederland reporting via Schuttelaar (https://www.schuttelaar-partners.com/update/week-of-the-food-banks/3197).

### NP-14. Food bank supply-side decline as retailers reduce waste

**Description.** Voedselbank Leiden and others report declining supermarket donations as retail chains become more effective at reducing overproduction. Offering a healthy "Wheel of Five" diet is now structurally harder even as demand grows.

**Cause.** ESG pressure on retailers incentivises waste reduction; just-in-time supply chains leave less surplus; regulatory nudges toward date-label clarity reduce the "unsellable but edible" pool; post-COVID shifts in grocery consumption.

**Effect.** Food banks are forced into a contradictory position — their environmental mission (waste reduction) is undermining their social mission (food aid). Recipients receive less variety and lower nutritional quality just as need rises.

**Sources.** Stichting De Groentedeler (link above); "Too big to fail: the resilience and entrenchment of food aid through food banks in the Netherlands" *Food Security* (https://edepot.wur.nl/566460).

### NP-15. Refugees cannot navigate digitalised Dutch bureaucracy

**Description.** Dutch public services are increasingly online, but newly arrived Syrians and other non-Dutch-speaking refugees face compounded barriers: complex procedures, language, digital literacy, and unfamiliar bureaucratic norms. They rely heavily on Arabic-speaking peer mentors.

**Cause.** "Bureaucratic digitalisation by default" design (DigiD, online municipal portals), insufficient multilingual interfaces, assumption of digital literacy, and concentration of information in Dutch-only formats.

**Effect.** Delays and failures in housing, school enrolment, healthcare access, tax filing, and benefit claims. Compounds the employment gap and slows integration trajectories that already underperform peer EU countries.

**Sources.** Social Inclusion journal (2026), "Navigating integration in the Netherlands" (https://www.cogitatiopress.com/socialinclusion/article/viewFile/10946/4877); Beyond the Horizon ISSG, "The integration challenges of Ukrainian refugees in the Netherlands" (https://behorizon.org/the-integration-challenges-of-ukrainian-refugees-in-the-netherlands-whats-next/).

### NP-16. Refugee labour-market underutilisation

**Description.** Only around 10% of recently arrived refugees and one third of former refugees are employed in the Netherlands; 73% of status-holders employed are part-time, 84% hold temporary contracts, and only 3% are self-employed.

**Cause.** Long asylum procedures under CEAS during which refugees cannot legally work; diploma and experience non-recognition; language requirement thresholds (B1); fragmented and minimal entrepreneurship support; spatial dispersal to reception centres far from labour markets.

**Effect.** Waste of human capital, long-term benefit dependency, 25% of refugees live at or below poverty level, social-integration outcomes degrade, and Dutch economy foregoes productivity.

**Sources.** MIT Solve, "Welcome NL / OpenEmbassy" (https://solve.mit.edu/solutions/7310); *Journal of International Migration and Integration* (2024), "The Poor Integration Results of the Common European Asylum System Compared to the Temporary Protection Directive" (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s12134-024-01155-8); Council of Europe Development Bank, "Pathway to integration and entrepreneurship for migrants" (https://coebank.org/en/news-and-publications/projects-focus/pathway-to-integration-and-entrepreneurship-migrants/).

### NP-17. Homeless EU citizens systematically denied shelter in Dutch cities

**Description.** Homeless EU citizens in the Netherlands are estimated to have grown from 2,500–3,000 in 2021 to at least 6,000 by autumn 2023. EU citizens form 3.6% of the Dutch population but approximately 75% of rough sleepers in Rotterdam and 80% in Amsterdam. A majority of municipalities denies them access to overnight shelters.

**Cause.** The administrative fiction of "non-entitlement" (*niet-rechthebbenden*); capacity and funding shortages; the absence of effective administrative-law procedures; pragmatism in Dutch public administration that does not always treat EU free-movement rights as binding.

**Effect.** People die on the streets of rich cities; the EU single market's promise of free movement rings hollow; frontline workers must triage by nationality instead of need.

**Sources.** Cambridge *German Law Journal*, "Mobilizing the Rights of Homeless EU Citizens in the Netherlands" (https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/german-law-journal/article/mobilizing-the-rights-of-homeless-eu-citizens-in-the-netherlands/43D7732B8E784CA17C9E5747944ED199).

### NP-18. Shelter safety deters use by the most vulnerable

**Description.** Many homeless people refuse shelter beds even during freezing weather because of drunkenness, fighting, sexual harassment, and theft among residents. Instead they sleep in streets, alleys, under bridges, or in forests.

**Cause.** Congregate-shelter design, insufficient staffing for safeguarding, co-location of people with active substance-use and untreated psychiatric conditions, limited Housing First capacity as an alternative, and absence of women-only or neurodivergent-friendly options.

**Effect.** Preventable cold-exposure deaths, worsening mental and physical health among rough sleepers, and increased call volume to emergency services.

**Sources.** Wikipedia, "Homelessness in the Netherlands" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homelessness_in_the_Netherlands); Housing First Europe, "Netherlands" (https://housingfirsteurope.eu/country/netherlands/); ICWA, "A deal too good to refuse: housing as health care" (https://www.icwa.org/dutch-housing-shelters/).

### NP-19. Loneliness and social isolation among older adults

**Description.** In the Netherlands, about 1 in 11 older adults is severely lonely; during COVID, emotional loneliness rose sharply. The post-2011 trend is upward even though NL starts from a lower base than southern/eastern Europe.

**Cause.** The 2007 household-help policy reform removed loneliness prevention as an explicit aim; demographic ageing (25% of population will be 65+ by 2040); urban isolation; digital divides for older adults; reduced neighbourhood attachment.

**Effect.** Loneliness is an independent predictor of morbidity and mortality; increases primary-care and emergency-service utilisation; reduces quality of life. Non-profits and municipalities run uncoordinated patchwork programs with inconsistent outcomes.

**Sources.** BMC Public Health (2018), "Does household help prevent loneliness among the elderly?" (https://bmcpublichealth.biomedcentral.com/articles/10.1186/s12889-018-6004-6); Longitudinal Aging Study Amsterdam in *The Journals of Gerontology* (https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8394819/); European Centre for Social Welfare Policy, "Social isolation and loneliness among older people in Europe" (https://www.euro.centre.org/projects/detail/4302).

### NP-20. Domestic-violence victims almost never reach support services

**Description.** Per CBS/WODC, only about 3% of domestic-violence victims in the Netherlands contact Veilig Thuis. Only one third discuss their situation with a social worker, psychologist, or GP. GREVIO has flagged that NL practices (e.g., reflection periods, barring-order scope) dissuade reporting.

**Cause.** Stigma and fear of consequences; Veilig Thuis is not perceived as anonymous; no 24/7 independent helpline; limited reach into non-Dutch-speaking communities; insufficient training of frontline professionals; cultural minimisation and gender-neutralisation of the problem.

**Effect.** Prolonged suffering, escalating violence, child exposure to abuse, femicide in severe cases, and inefficient deployment of emergency-service resources because cases arrive only in crisis.

**Sources.** NL Times, "Only 3% of domestic violence victims seek help" (https://nltimes.nl/2025/05/11/3-domestic-violence-victims-netherlands-seek-help-support-agency); Council of Europe, GREVIO first thematic report on NL (https://www.coe.int/en/web/portal/-/violence-against-women-netherlands-legal-and-policy-changes-praised); Mellaard & van Meijl (2021), "Domestic violence policies in the Netherlands: A regime of deficiency" *Anthropology & Medicine* (https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1463499620958857).

## Part II — Emergency Services Problems

### ES-01. Ambulance response-time standard breached at rising rate

**Description.** Dutch law requires ambulances to reach emergencies within 15 minutes in 95% of cases. In 2023, 9.3% of urgent response times exceeded 15 minutes; RTL's analysis of ~3 million responses from 2019–2023 found the standard was not met once in any of those years.

**Cause.** Increased demand, staffing shortages, more traffic and speed-reducing measures, fewer permanently staffed ambulance posts, and residents living farther from posts (2.1% of Dutch residents unreachable within 15 minutes in 2023 vs. 0.5% in 2019).

**Effect.** Delayed care worsens outcomes in time-critical conditions (cardiac arrest, stroke, major trauma). 63 villages and districts experience late arrivals more often than not — a structural inequity.

**Sources.** NL Times, "Ambulance response times rising, especially in rural areas" (https://nltimes.nl/2025/02/21/ambulance-response-times-rising-especially-rural-areas); Wikipedia, "Emergency medical services in the Netherlands" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergency_medical_services_in_the_Netherlands).

---

### ES-02. Rural and remote ambulance access gap

**Description.** Beyond the headline response-time number, 63 specific villages and districts in the Netherlands receive late ambulance arrivals more often than not. The gap has grown steadily since 2019.

**Cause.** Post consolidation (fewer permanent rural posts for economic efficiency), road infrastructure changes, population distribution shifts, and staff preference for urban posts.

**Effect.** Two-tier emergency care based on postcode; worse outcomes in cardiac arrest, stroke, and obstetric emergencies in rural areas; erosion of trust in the universalist promise of Dutch healthcare.

**Sources.** NL Times (link above); RIVM data released under Open Government Act as reported by RTL Nieuws.

---

### ES-03. Language barriers in 112 calls

**Description.** When calling 112 for help abroad, 28% of callers experience language problems. In NL specifically, non-Dutch speakers often struggle to communicate symptoms and locations, leading to delays in recognition and dispatch. U.S. data suggests limited-English-proficiency callers wait 5–10 minutes for a native-language interpreter.

**Cause.** Call-centre staffing based on national language competence; multilingual software support is not the sector standard; interpreter-relay workflows add latency; callers code-switch under stress.

**Effect.** Measurable delays in cardiac-arrest recognition; lower rates of bystander CPR for LEP callers; worse outcomes in cases where minutes matter. U.S. regulators estimate a one-minute reduction in 9-1-1 response could save up to 10,000 lives annually.

**Sources.** EENA, *Multilingual Emergency Calls* (https://eena.org/knowledge-hub/documents/multilingual-112-calls/); Global Rescue, "European Emergency Numbers and Response Reputation" (https://www.globalrescue.com/common/blog/detail/european-emergency-numbers/); Carbyne, "Breaking Language Barriers in Emergency Calls" (https://carbyne.com/blogs/breaking-language-barriers-in-emergency-calls-the-need-for-immediate-language-identification-and-translation/); Bradley & Rea, PubMed 21388734 (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/21388734/).

### ES-04. Dispatcher recognition of out-of-hospital cardiac arrest

**Description.** In a well-performing dispatch centre, ~80% of cardiac arrests are correctly identified over the phone; elsewhere, recognition rates fall as low as 20%. Even in strong systems, the median time to recognise arrest can exceed 75 seconds.

**Cause.** Agonal breathing misinterpreted as breathing; caller emotional state; protocol-driven question flow that delays "is the patient breathing normally?"; seizure-like activity at arrest onset (shown to delay recognition); free-text vs. structured dispatch coding.

**Effect.** Every minute of delay in defibrillation reduces survival by 7–10%. Missed recognition means no T-CPR instructions and no volunteer-responder activation, collapsing the entire chain of survival.

**Sources.** Scientific Statement from AHA on lay-responder experience in OHCA, *Circulation* (https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/CIR.0000000000001054); Lewis et al., PubMed 23983252 (https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/23983252/); Culley et al., "Seizure-like presentation in OHCA creates barriers to dispatch recognition" *Resuscitation* (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0300957220302756).

---

### ES-05. Dispatcher-assisted CPR refusal and delay

**Description.** Even when cardiac arrest is correctly identified, many callers refuse or delay compressions. Global DA-CPR uptake remains suboptimal; median time from call to first chest compression is ~176 seconds (almost 3 minutes). Barriers include "could not move the patient" and caller refusal.

**Cause.** Caller emotional stress, physical inability to move the patient to the floor, fear of causing harm (myth that CPR will injure the patient), fear of legal liability, lack of prior training, and awkward phone instruction scripts.

**Effect.** Compression-free minutes directly translate to worse neurologic outcomes. Bystander CPR roughly doubles survival; without it, most arrests are not survivable.

**Sources.** Internal and Emergency Medicine (2025), scoping review on DA-CPR (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s11739-025-03991-7); *Resuscitation* (2020), bystander emotional stress and DA-CPR (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10656878/); Linderoth et al., *Resuscitation* (2015), CCTV + audio analysis of OHCA (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0300957215002464).

---

### ES-06. Seizure-like activity at arrest onset confuses recognition

**Description.** Roughly 30% of arrest victims exhibit seizure-like activity or agonal breathing at collapse. Dispatch protocols that wait for convulsions to end before asking about breathing delay CPR instructions significantly.

**Cause.** Protocol design intended to prevent CPR on seizing epilepsy patients misfires when the seizure is actually hypoxic. Bystander vocabulary ("he's twitching") is ambiguous. Training does not adequately cover the overlap.

**Effect.** Missed recognition → no T-CPR → no volunteer-responder activation. A specific, well-studied failure mode with clear impact on survival.

**Sources.** Culley et al., "Seizure-like presentation in OHCA" *Resuscitation* (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0300957220302756); Berdowski et al., *Resuscitation* 2009 (cited in NCBI Bookshelf NBK321502, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK321502/).

### ES-07. First-responder PTSD at ~1.5× the general-population rate

**Description.** Approximately 30% of first responders develop behavioural-health conditions (depression, PTSD), compared with 20% in the general population. 85% of first responders report mental-health symptoms. Depression and PTSD are five times more common in first responders than the general population by some measures.

**Cause.** Repeated, cumulative exposure to traumatic events as a normative part of the job; organisational stigma around help-seeking; irregular shifts and sleep disruption; limited access to culturally-competent trauma care; underfunded employee-assistance programs.

**Effect.** Higher suicide rates (firefighters and police officers more likely to die by suicide than in the line of duty); attrition (average EMT tenure ~5 years); degraded on-duty decision-making; secondary trauma to families.

**Sources.** SAMHSA, *First Responders: Behavioral Health Concerns, Emergency Response, and Trauma* (https://www.samhsa.gov/sites/default/files/dtac/supplementalresearchbulletin-firstresponders-may2018.pdf); *Journal of Anxiety Disorders* (2025), meta-analysis of global PTSD prevalence in first responders (https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0272735825000893); Relief Mental Health, "First Responders and Mental Health" (https://reliefmh.com/blog/first-responders-mental-health/).

### ES-08. EMS/paramedic operational burnout and attrition

**Description.** Nearly half of U.S. firefighters experience burnout; EMTs average ~5 years on the job. Nearly identical dynamics apply in European systems. Insomnia and nightmares in paramedics correlate with medical error.

**Cause.** Long shifts (12–24 hours), unpredictable exposure to traumatic incidents, absence of consistent decompression rituals, critical-incident stress that is not always debriefed, and limited leadership investment in wellness.

**Effect.** Direct link to patient safety (errors under fatigue), workforce shortage feedback loop (stress causes attrition causes more overtime for remainders), and rising workers' compensation claims.

**Sources.** EMS1, "Ways to avoid paramedic, firefighter and EMS burnout" (https://www.ems1.com/health-wellness/ways-to-avoid-paramedic-firefighter-and-ems-burnout); *PMC* (2019), "Conceptualization, Assessment, and Treatment of Traumatic Stress in First Responders" (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC6624844/).

### ES-09. Dispatch overtriage strains limited ambulance resources

**Description.** A 2021–2023 Quebec study showed 74.5% overtriage: three quarters of "urgent" dispatches were judged non-urgent on scene by paramedics. 86.1% of P3 dispatches were classified non-urgent. Abdominal pain, falls, and psychiatric complaints showed overtriage >90%.

**Cause.** Risk-averse dispatch protocols (better to overtriage than miss a critical case), symptom-based flowcharts that map ambiguous complaints to high priority, caller escalation under stress, and absence of secondary-triage review.

**Effect.** Urgent units occupied on non-urgent calls; "level 0" ambulance coverage moments where no units are available for genuine emergencies; lights-and-sirens road-accident risk; paramedic burnout.

**Sources.** *Scandinavian Journal of Trauma, Resuscitation and Emergency Medicine* (2025), Quebec MPDS study (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s13049-025-01410-6); Wikipedia, "Triage" (https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triage).

### ES-10. Dispatch undertriage delays care for time-critical patients

**Description.** Undertriage rates range from 5% to ~28% in different systems. In Swedish and Norwegian studies, allergic reactions, diabetic problems, and heart conditions are undertriaged >10%. Undertriage directly delays care for patients with time-sensitive conditions.

**Cause.** Information gathering limitations over the phone, protocol rigidities, workload-driven shortcuts during peak hours, caller under-communication of severity, and limited ability for dispatchers to consult colleagues or physicians under load.

**Effect.** Delayed arrival of appropriate resources, patient deterioration during wait, and worse survival in STEMI, stroke, anaphylaxis, and sepsis.

**Sources.** Møller et al. (2018), *BMC Emergency Medicine* (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC5747276/); Elfström et al. (2022), *BMC Emergency Medicine* (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8744325/); *BMC Emergency Medicine* (2024), EMCC stroke decision-making (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12873-024-01129-0).

### ES-11. Mental-health crisis defaulted to police response

**Description.** About 6–10% of all police encounters involve people with serious mental illness. When coded inclusively, up to 19% of 9-1-1 call volume is behavioural-health related. In the U.S., a quarter of fatal police shootings involve people with behavioural-health conditions. Dutch police similarly respond to *verwarde personen* calls in increasing numbers.

**Cause.** Deinstitutionalisation without commensurate community-care expansion; 24/7 availability of police but not of mobile crisis teams; dispatch systems that lack a clinical routing option; cultural reflex of police-as-default responders.

**Effect.** Preventable deaths and injuries, retraumatisation of people in crisis, arrests instead of care, police time diverted from crime response, and paramedic exposure to unsafe scenes.

**Sources.** APA Monitor (2021), "Building mental health into emergency responses" (https://www.apa.org/monitor/2021/07/emergency-responses); Vera Institute, "We Need to Think Beyond Police in Mental Health Crises" (https://www.vera.org/news/we-need-to-think-beyond-police-in-mental-health-crises); Congressional Research Service, *Issues in Law Enforcement Reform: Responding to Mental Health Crises* (https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R47285).

### ES-12. AED temporal-access gap (off-hours and weekends)

**Description.** A Toronto study found 73.5% of registered AEDs are not open 24/7, and 28.6% are closed on weekends. 21.5% of public OHCAs near an AED occurred when that AED was actually inaccessible. Spatiotemporal optimisation could improve coverage by 25.3%.

**Cause.** AED deployment registries assume 24/7 availability; host buildings lock up at night; no centralised operational-hours database; no integration with dispatch systems that could route bystanders to currently-accessible units.

**Effect.** "Coverage" figures systematically overstate real accessibility; OHCAs at night — when survival is worst — are the least likely to receive timely defibrillation; a specific, technical gap with clear leverage.

**Sources.** Sun et al. (2016), *JACC*, "Overcoming Spatial and Temporal Barriers to Public Access Defibrillators" (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4992180/); Karlsson et al. (2019), *Resuscitation* (referenced in PMC 8136701, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8136701/).

### ES-13. Residential cardiac-arrest coverage gap

**Description.** The majority of OHCAs occur in private residences, but these are the locations with the *lowest* AED coverage. Existing PAD programs focus on public spaces, leaving home-based arrests effectively uncovered.

**Cause.** Historical program logic ("higher-density public traffic = more shockable rhythms to target"); private AEDs are expensive and undiscoverable by neighbours; no integration with dispatch that could route bystanders to nearby home AEDs; limited volunteer-responder networks for residential zones.

**Effect.** OHCAs at home have substantially worse survival than public arrests. The Dutch HartslagNu / 6-minutes-zone system is one of the global leaders here but still has gaps, especially in rural areas and apartment complexes.

**Sources.** PMC 8136701 (2021), "How to improve automated external defibrillator placement for out-of-hospital cardiac arrests" (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8136701/); AHA AED Symposium, *JAHA* 2025 (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12132870/).

### ES-14. Multi-agency information silos in disaster response

**Description.** EU disaster-response exercises (e.g., the large 4-day UK/Cyprus/Hungary/Italy exercise) consistently show that barriers within and between countries are prevalent during high-uncertainty windows: language, technology incompatibilities, different roles, and co-location failures. Agencies run on disparate servers with poor interoperability.

**Cause.** Each agency builds its own CAD/IT system to its own procurement cycle; data-protection rules restrict cross-agency sharing; absence of standard operating procedures across civil-protection actors; political and juridical barriers to electronic information flow.

**Effect.** Delayed situational awareness, duplicated deployments, missed vulnerable populations, and — in large incidents like MH17 or floods — documented information-sharing failures that cost time and potentially lives.

**Sources.** *Journal of Emergency Management* (2019), facilitators and barriers to information sharing (https://wmpllc.org/ojs/index.php/jem/article/view/2682); *International Journal of Disaster Risk Science* (2020), Barriers and Facilitators in Interorganizational Disaster Response (https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s13753-020-00249-y); CORDIS IDIRA project report (https://cordis.europa.eu/project/id/261726/reporting).

### ES-15. Cross-border emergency coordination in the Benelux / EU

**Description.** Pan-European civil protection relies on the EU Civil Protection Mechanism, but cross-border disasters (Ahrtal 2021 floods; 2022 Greek-Albanian-Bulgarian wildfires) reveal persistent coordination gaps between neighbouring 112 centres, fire services, and water management authorities.

**Cause.** National SOPs are not harmonised; radio and data systems are not interoperable; different legal mandates for mayors/governors; language barriers between responders; incompatible forms and forms-of-authority for deploying assets across borders.

**Effect.** Delay in mutual aid, redundant requests for resources, inconsistent civilian messaging along borders, and erosion of the EU single-civil-protection space that the Mechanism is supposed to create.

**Sources.** CORDIS, DIREKTION project (https://cordis.europa.eu/project/id/101121249); European Commission MEDiate analysis (https://open-research-europe.ec.europa.eu/articles/5-97/v1/xml?article_uuid=01689bf4-554c-4999-b50a-24fdf6a99e06).

### ES-16. Elderly and disabled residents disadvantaged in flood evacuation

**Description.** In flood-prone southern NL, evacuation tools are still being developed (MICD/TU Delft project). Elderly and mobility-limited residents frequently cannot evacuate on short timelines and need tailored, door-to-door intervention plans. Historical floods show comms collapse when it's needed most.

**Cause.** NL-Alert and sirens assume functioning smartphones and hearing; municipalities lack up-to-date registries of mobility-limited residents; caregivers are uncoordinated; hospitals and care homes have evacuation plans but community-dwelling elderly often don't.

**Effect.** Disproportionate casualty distribution among the elderly in floods and fires; trauma to rescue workers who find preventable fatalities; erosion of trust in public warning systems.

**Sources.** MICD / TU Delft Flood Evacuation project (https://micd.tudelftcampus.nl/projects/flood-evacuation/); Utrecht Law Review (2022), Dutch flood risk management (https://utrechtlawreview.org/articles/10.36633/ulr.860); *Journal of Public Health Research* case study on flood evacuation challenges (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9525901/).

### ES-17. Emotionally-stressed callers degrade cardiac-arrest call quality

**Description.** Callers in extreme distress frequently cannot follow dispatcher instructions, struggle to assess the patient, and report misleading barriers ("I can't move them"). Observational analysis in Copenhagen shows ~32% of callers are "emotionally stressed" and delays are significant.

**Cause.** Lack of dispatcher training in de-escalation under load; absence of structured calming protocols; high background noise at the scene; caller identity (family member, neighbour) affects distress level; no video-first workflows that could reduce verbal overhead.

**Effect.** Longer time to first compression; more missed recognitions; dispatcher secondary-trauma from high-emotion calls; worse outcomes especially for family-witnessed OHCA at home.

**Sources.** *Resuscitation* (2023), bystander emotional stress in DA-CPR (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10656878/); Perera et al. (2023), PLOS One, "If you miss that first step in the chain of survival" (PMID 36913363).

### ES-18. Bystander AED use remains extremely low despite deployment

**Description.** Despite three decades of PAD programs, AEDs are used in only about 4% of OHCA cases in the U.S. Even where AEDs are physically near, bystanders hesitate to retrieve or use them.

**Cause.** Legal-liability anxiety despite Good Samaritan protections; lack of awareness of AED locations; intimidation by the device; absence of dispatch-driven routing to nearest device; sparse refresher training; specific demographic groups (women, minorities) are under-trained.

**Effect.** The chain of survival breaks at link 3 even when links 1 and 2 succeed. Dutch HartslagNu's volunteer-responder model shows what's possible (>50% AED application before EMS arrival) but most countries lag far behind.

**Sources.** AHA, *Circulation*, "Part 4: The Automated External Defibrillator" (https://www.ahajournals.org/doi/10.1161/circ.102.suppl_1.i-60); *JAHA* 2025, AED Symposium (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12132870/); *Cureus* systematic review (2023) (https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10676231/).

### ES-19. Early wildfire / water-incident detection in sparsely-monitored areas

**Description.** Wildfires are now a NL/Benelux concern (Veluwe, Limburg moorlands) as well as a Southern European one. Water-incident detection (dike breaches, nuisance flooding) still relies on manual inspection in many Dutch waterschappen. AI-sensor networks and satellite-based early warning exist but deployment is uneven.

**Cause.** Cost of sensor networks; overlapping mandates between waterschappen, Rijkswaterstaat, and municipalities; limited open-data access to real-time water levels outside crisis windows; satellite wildfire feeds not yet operationally integrated for small domestic fires.

**Effect.** Fires and water incidents escalate before being recognised; evacuation windows shrink; critical infrastructure (dykes, substations) fails before redundancies activate. Demonstrated elsewhere that 37-minute head-starts from AI sensors change outcomes materially.

**Sources.** DHS S&T, "Technology to Reduce the Impacts of Wildfires" (https://www.dhs.gov/science-and-technology/technology-reduce-impacts-wildfires); AEM, "Wildfire Detection and Risk Monitoring Systems" (https://aem.eco/solution/wildfire-risk-management/); OroraTech product documentation (https://ororatech.com/).

### ES-20. Domestic-violence cases rarely reach police in time to prevent escalation

**Description.** Although Veilig Thuis and 112 are available, domestic-violence calls tend to reach police only at crisis moments. The GREVIO evaluation of NL highlights under-use of emergency barring orders, insufficient risk assessment at first police contact, and barriers to victim help-seeking.

**Cause.** Victim barriers (see NP-20); police first-responder protocols designed around evidentiary thresholds rather than risk-assessment; inconsistent use of the LINK risk tool; coordination between Veilig Thuis, OM, and police is fragmented at the municipal level.

**Effect.** Repeat call-outs to the same addresses; escalation to severe assault or femicide; children exposed to chronic violence; emergency service time spent on reactive rather than preventive work.

**Sources.** Council of Europe GREVIO thematic report on NL (https://www.coe.int/en/web/portal/-/violence-against-women-netherlands-legal-and-policy-changes-praised); Baker McKenzie Resource Hub, *Fighting Domestic Violence — The Netherlands* (https://resourcehub.bakermckenzie.com/en/resources/fighting-domestic-violence/europe/the-netherlands/); *Anthropology & Medicine* (2021), "Domestic violence policies in the Netherlands" (https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1463499620958857).
